Content Design · B2B Fintech

Signer Management — Citigroup

Brought in mid-project as the first content designer for a platform being built for treasury managers across the globe. Designs were already underway. The brief was to bring content rigor to a complex, multi-jurisdiction document flow being moved online for the first time.

The platform — Documents screen (original)
The Brief

Citi was moving a complex, paper-heavy treasury process online for the first time. I was handed a single screen to begin with, and worked on a significant portion of the platform from there.

Early user interviews on the initial screens had flagged content as a problem area. That was the signal that prompted bringing in a UX writer.

What Was Actually Happening

Three things ran through every decision. Lack of content standards for the platform, no agreed capitalization, no naming conventions, nothing. The users were treasury managers across the globe, many of whom did not have English as their first language. The flow itself was high-stakes; compliance-critical decisions with no tolerance for ambiguous labels.

Conciseness Cutting without losing meaning
Page subtitle
Before
Please provide the following documents for various signers and entities listed on this request. 29 words. "Please" adds no value, especially when other Citi products showed no signs of an extremely respectful tone. The screen's context and the upcoming flow set the required context. Subtitle only needed to tell them what to do next.
After
Provide the following documents to process the request. Action-oriented from the first word. Character count reduced by ~35%. "Please" carries no functional meaning in a B2B product used by treasury managers, and leading with a verb shifts the tone to directive, which is appropriate for a task-heavy professional tool.
Clarity Naming things for what they actually are
Document count label + column header + status label
Before
5/5 Documents required When the count changes to 4/5, "required" implies only 4 are required — not that 4 of 5 remain.
After
5/5 documents pending "Pending" holds its meaning accurately across all states and was validated in user interviews.
Before
Approval Level Required Users in interviews didn't know what "level" referred to. The label was flagged directly as unclear.
After
Documentation and Verification Type Required The column captures two things: the format a document must be in (digital, physical) and the verification method required (e.g. digital signatures, apostille). "Authorization" and "Approval" were both rejected — approval happens after documents are submitted to Citi, not before. "Verification" is more accurate for what this column actually communicates.
Before — Status labels
Upload required  |  Physical doc required "Upload required" has two problems: it assumes the document will always be submitted digitally (a physical document cannot be uploaded), and it states a requirement rather than a status. The column is called Status — the values should reflect state, not obligation.
After
Pending  |  Received Channel-agnostic and genuinely status-indicating. Whether a document arrives digitally or as a physical copy, "Pending" and "Received" accurately reflect the state of the document at any point in the flow.
Readability Choosing words a global audience can parse
Inline link + certification tag
Before
Show me other alternatives "Alternatives" has a lower readability score. High character count for an inline link.
After
Show other options "Options" is more widely understood globally and makes the phrase concise without losing intent.
Before
Wet Signatures Heuristically and semantically unclear for non-native English speakers. "Wet" is not an intuitive qualifier for a signature in many languages.
After
Physical signature Directly communicates the format: a signature made on paper. Clear and interpretable across language backgrounds.
Content Strategy Writing for user intent, not system logic
Toggle label — two directions explored for A/B testing
Before
I want to consolidate and provide minimal documents "Consolidate" describes what the system does, not what the user wants. UX copy should communicate what the user needs to do, not how the system will handle it.
Option A — Intent-led
I want to provide minimal documents Leads with the user's intent. The user isn't thinking about how the system will process it, but the fact that they just want fewer documents. This option mirrors that mental model directly.
or
Option B — Outcome-led
Show universally accepted options Leads with what is possible. More formal, less personalized; the system is offering something rather than the user requesting it. The A/B test was designed to answer: do users respond better to copy that mirrors their intent (Option A) or copy that describes what's available (Option B)?
Consistency Setting a capitalization standard
Capitalization inconsistencies across labels
Before — No consistent rule
Digital Signatures  |  Simple Copy  |  Wet Signatures  |  Apostilled  |  Physical required Title case and sentence case coexist without logic — sometimes within the same row. No standard had been set.
After — Sentence case throughout
Digital signatures  |  Simple copy  |  Wet signatures  |  Apostilled  |  Physical required Sentence case applied consistently across all labels. Took the initiative to standardize capitalization formats across labels for at least this product.
The Outcome

The project was paused before launch, so there's no usage data. What exists is interview validation: most copy changes were tested with treasury managers and confirmed to reduce confusion, particularly around terminology that was flagged as unclear in earlier sessions. The capitalization standard and naming conventions were documented and handed off as a foundation for any future work on the platform.
